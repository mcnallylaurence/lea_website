## The Brand Voice in One Sentence

**Ladybird sounds like a trusted friend who has actually done this — warm, expert, and genuinely excited for American couples planning an Irish wedding 4,000 miles from home.**

---

## Core Voice Attributes

### 1. **Authenticity First**
- We write from lived experience: Yasmeen and Laurence got married in Ireland themselves. This is not marketing copy. It is the foundation.
- Every piece of writing should feel like a real person sharing real knowledge, not a corporation speaking at you.
- Avoid jargon, corporate buzzwords, industry speak.
- **Example:**
  - ✗ "Our comprehensive destination wedding coordination services leverage synergistic planning frameworks..."
  - ✓ "We handle every vendor so you don't have to. One invoice. One point of contact. That's it."

### 2. **Warm & Encouraging**
- Couples are planning 4,000 miles away. They are nervous. We make them feel safe.
- Use inclusive language: "we," "let's," "together."
- Acknowledge the emotions: the overwhelm, the excitement, the risk of doing something unconventional.
- **Example:**
  - ✗ "Complete your wedding booking."
  - ✓ "Let's make this real."

### 3. **Clear & Practical**
- American couples need clarity. They don't know Irish wedding law, vendor names, or how to manage an international wedding.
- Explain what things cost, how long they take, what paperwork is needed. No hiding in vagueness.
- Use short sentences. Avoid nested clauses.
- **Example:**
  - ✗ "The legal requirements surrounding ceremonial proceedings in the Republic of Ireland, which necessitate advance notice to the registrar, vary based on the nature of the ceremony and the citizenship of the participants."
  - ✓ "You'll need to register with the Irish registrar three months before your wedding. We'll guide you through every step."

### 4. **Slightly Playful, Never Cheesy**
- We're planning something joyful. The voice should reflect that.
- Use light humor when it lands. Avoid forced puns or "cute couple" language.
- Trust the reader to appreciate subtlety.
- **Example:**
  - ✗ "Let's have some fun planning your big day, you crazy lovebirds!"
  - ✓ "Your wedding is weird and wonderful. We get it. Let's lean into that."

### 5. **Honest About the Differences**
- We don't hide the fact that marrying in Ireland is different from marrying at home.
- We don't pretend to be something we're not (Irish-based, for example).
- We lean into what makes us different: US-based, USD contract, zero FX risk.
- **Example:**
  - ✗ "Experience authentic Irish traditions with our worldwide planning network..."
  - ✓ "You're marrying in Ireland, not marrying like you're in Ireland. We help you navigate what that means."

## Tone Guidelines by Context

### **Casual Contexts** (Blog posts, social media, welcome email)
- Conversational, friendly, personal pronouns
- Short paragraphs. Shorter sentences.
- Use contractions: "you're," "we'll," "don't."
- Share vulnerability alongside expertise.

### **Transactional Contexts** (Pricing page, package descriptions, legal guidance)
- Still warm, but clearer and more structured.
- Use bulleted lists for clarity.
- Explain the "why" behind each inclusion.
- Bold key differentiators.

### **High-Stakes Contexts** (Legal guidance, contract info, ceremony rules)
- Professional but still approachable.
- Expertise without condescension.
- Detailed and thorough, but readable.
- Acknowledge complexity honestly.

## What We Don't Sound Like

### ❌ Corporate or Formal
- ✗ "We are pleased to present our comprehensive wedding planning services..."
- ✓ "Here's what we do."

### ❌ Overly Poetic or Flowery
- ✗ "Where ancient Celtic magic meets modern love..."
- ✓ "You're getting married in one of the world's most beautiful places."

### ❌ Pretending to Be Irish
- ✗ "Top o' the mornin'... let's get your craic on!"
- ✓ "Here's what you need to know about Irish ceremony traditions."

### ❌ Condescending or Over-Explaining
- ✗ "Now don't worry, getting married internationally is super simple for us wedding experts!"
- ✓ "This sounds complicated. We've handled it hundreds of times. Let us walk you through it."

### ❌ Generic Couple-Speak
- ✗ "Your fairytale awaits!"
- ✓ "You're doing something bold. Let's make sure it works."

## Key Phrases & Patterns to Use

### **Opening Hooks**
- "Here's the thing..."
- "You're probably wondering..."
- "Let's be honest..."
- "Here's what nobody tells you about..."

### **Reassurance Phrases**
- "We've got this."
- "We handle the stress. You get the joy."
- "One less thing to worry about."
- "You're not the first couple to ask this. Here's what we've learned..."

### **Clarity Phrases**
- "In plain English..."
- "Here's the breakdown..."
- "What this actually means..."
- "The short version: ..."

### **Confidence Phrases**
- "Based on 200+ real weddings..."
- "We've seen every scenario."
- "This is exactly why we exist."
- "Trust us. We know Ireland."

## Common Words & Phrases to Avoid

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "Leverage," "synergize," "streamline" | "Use," "bring together," "simplify" |
| "Curate," "artisan," "bespoke" (overused) | "Select," "handpicked," "custom" |
| "Journey," "adventure" (in wedding context) | "Plan," "celebrate," "experience" |
| "Magical," "enchanted," "fairytale" | "Beautiful," "meaningful," "real" |
| "Em-dash" (—) | Use en-dash (–) or rephrase |
| "And/or" | Choose one or rewrite |
| "Which," "that" (in buried clauses) | Rephrase with shorter sentences |

## The Founder Story — Use Liberally

**The Origin:**
Yasmeen and Laurence met at a bar called Ladybird in Atlanta. They fell in love. Then they planned their own wedding in Ireland — from the US, without help. They figured it out. They built Ladybird Ever After because every couple deserves someone who has actually done this.

**Why It Matters:**
This story is real. It is verifiable. It resonates with American couples. Use it when:
- Building trust on a homepage
- Explaining why we understand their specific pain points
- Differentiating from competitors
- Humanizing the brand on social or in a blog post

**How to Tell It:**
- Short version: 1–2 sentences. "We planned our own Irish wedding. Now we help others do the same."
- Medium version: 2–3 paragraphs. Include the Atlanta bar, the long-distance planning, the insight that sparked the business.
- Long version: 4–5 paragraphs. Include personality, the emotional journey, specific details about what was hard.

## Handling Complexity: Legal, Pricing, Logistics

When explaining complex topics (Irish legal requirements, pricing differences, vendor management), use this structure:

1. **Acknowledge the complexity:** "This gets complicated."
2. **Provide the practical truth:** "Here's what you need to know."
3. **Break it into steps:** Use numbered lists or short paragraphs.
4. **Offer reassurance:** "We handle the logistics. You just show up."

**Example:**
"Getting legally married in Ireland is different from getting married at home. You'll need to register with the Irish registrar at least three months in advance. The process involves paperwork, proof of residence, and some waiting. Sounds complicated? We've guided 200+ couples through it. Here's how it actually works..."

## Tone Checklist Before Publishing

Ask these questions of every piece of writing:

- [ ] Could Yasmeen or Laurence have said this in a conversation?
- [ ] Does it sound like a real person, not a corporation?
- [ ] Is it clear to someone who has never done this before?
- [ ] Does it explain the "why," not just the "what"?
- [ ] Would you feel reassured if you were the couple reading it?
- [ ] Did we avoid corporate jargon and flowery language?
- [ ] Does it subtly reinforce our competitive advantages (US-based, USD, founder story)?
- [ ] Is the sentence structure clear and readable?

Electricity and Plugs

Ireland uses the Type G plug-three rectangular prongs, the same as the UK. If you have been to the UK, you already know what to bring.

More importantly: Ireland's power grid runs on 220 volts. A standard American appliance runs on 110 volts. If you plug a 110-volt hair straightener, curling iron, or travel steamer into an Irish outlet with only a plastic plug adapter-no voltage conversion-it will catch fire or melt.

Before you pack any electrical appliance, check the label. If it says "100-240V" or "110-240V," it is dual voltage and safe to use with a simple plug adapter. If it says only "110V" or "120V," leave it at home or buy a dedicated voltage converter (not just an adapter).

This is especially important for hair tools and steamers. It is less of a concern for phone chargers and laptop chargers, which are almost universally dual voltage-but check the label on yours to be sure.

Staying Connected

Before you leave the US, call your cell carrier and ask about an international day pass for Ireland, or buy an eSIM through an app like Airalo before you travel. An eSIM gives you a local data plan without swapping physical SIM cards.

Download WhatsApp before you arrive. Every business in Ireland uses it: taxi drivers, florists, hair and makeup artists, restaurants, and tour guides all expect to communicate via WhatsApp. If you need to reach anyone local, that is how it happens. We will use it to communicate with you on the ground too.

Tipping

Tipping culture in Ireland is different from the US. The expectation is not 20-25%.

At restaurants: Check your bill first. Many Irish restaurants include a service charge of 10 to 12.5%. If it is already there, you do not need to add more. If there is no service charge, a 10% tip for good sit-down dinner service is considered generous.

At pubs: You do not tip the bartender for pints. You pay the price of the drink. That is it.

The Guinness Rule

If you order a pint of Guinness, the bartender will fill it roughly three-quarters of the way and then set it on the bar and walk away. Do not touch it. Do not pick it up. Do not ask if it is finished. It needs to settle-usually about two minutes-before they top it off and hand it to you. This is not indifference. It is the correct way to pour a Guinness, and you will be judged warmly if you know to wait.

Payments and Cash

Cards are accepted almost everywhere in Ireland. American Express is less widely accepted than Visa or Mastercard. Use a card with no foreign transaction fees-there are several options in the US market-and pay in euros rather than accepting the card terminal's offer to convert to dollars. The exchange rate offered at the terminal is almost always worse than your card's rate.

Bring some cash. Smaller markets, some car parks, and occasional rural vendors may be cash-only. A modest amount in euros will cover any gaps.
